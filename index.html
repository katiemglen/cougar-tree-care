Why EAB Matters in Fargo-Moorhead

Emerald Ash Borer is an invasive beetle that has devastated ash tree populations across the Midwest, and it is now confirmed in North Dakota. Green ash and black ash are the most vulnerable species — and green ash is one of the most commonly planted trees in the Fargo-Moorhead area.

Signs of EAB infestation to watch for:

  • D-shaped exit holes in the bark (about 1/8 inch wide)
  • Canopy dieback starting from the top of the tree
  • Bark splitting, sometimes revealing S-shaped larval galleries underneath
  • Increased woodpecker activity — they feed on EAB larvae
  • Epicormic sprouting (new shoots growing from the trunk and large branches)
  • Thinning or yellowing leaves in the upper crown

If you have ash trees on your property, get them assessed before it’s too late. Early detection gives you options — treatment or planned removal — instead of emergency removal of a dead or hazardous tree.

Tree Removals

You noticed it last summer — a dead limb hanging over the garage, bark peeling in sheets, the trunk leaning a little more after every wind event. You kept thinking it would be fine for another year. Then October hit, and your neighbor’s ash tree dropped a limb through their roof. Suddenly “another year” didn’t feel like a plan anymore.

When You Need This

  • Dead, dying, or structurally compromised trees threatening your home, garage, fence, or power lines
  • Storm-damaged trees that can’t be safely pruned back to health
  • Trees causing foundation issues, sewer line intrusion, or sidewalk heaving
  • Lot prep before construction, additions, or landscaping projects
  • Hazard trees flagged by your insurance company or city inspector

What We Do

Every removal starts with a plan. We assess the lean, the surroundings, the weight distribution, and the landing zone before a single cut is made. For tight spaces — between houses, over pools, near power lines — we use sectional rigging to lower limbs in controlled pieces. Nothing free-falls onto your property.

Our crew runs Husqvarna and STIHL saws, bucket trucks for canopy access, and grapple trucks for fast, clean log handling. On large or complex removals, crane-assisted lifts take the risk out of the equation entirely.

Fargo-area species like cottonwood, American elm, green ash, and box elder each have their own quirks — brittle wood, shallow roots, unpredictable grain. An experienced crew knows the difference. An inexperienced one learns the hard way, usually at your property’s expense.

Cleanup & Winter Advantage

Full cleanup is included on every job — brush chipped on-site, logs hauled, yard raked. If you want firewood, we’ll buck and stack it. And here’s something most homeowners don’t realize: winter removals on frozen ground mean virtually zero yard damage. No ruts, no torn-up turf. Your lawn comes through looking the same as before we showed up.

The difference between a removal that goes well and one that doesn’t usually comes down to planning, equipment, and the crew doing the work. Companies that have been running the same team for years develop a shorthand — they anticipate problems before they happen. That’s not something you can hire off a job board in April. It’s built over time, job after job, season after season. Ask how long a company’s crew has been together. The answer tells you a lot.

Tree Pruning

That big bur oak in the backyard — it’s been there longer than the house. It shades the patio all summer and looks great from the street. But you’ve noticed some dead branches up top, a couple of limbs rubbing together, and lower branches scraping the roof when the wind picks up. It doesn’t need to come down. It needs someone who knows how to take care of it.

When You Need This

  • Deadwood accumulating in the canopy — a falling branch hazard during storms
  • Branches contacting your roof, siding, or gutter line
  • Overgrown canopy blocking light, airflow, or views
  • Young trees that need structural pruning to develop strong branch architecture
  • Fruit trees or ornamentals that haven’t been maintained in years

What We Do

All pruning follows ISA (International Society of Arboriculture) standards — meaning proper cut placement, no flush cuts, no stub cuts, and absolutely no topping. Topping is the single most harmful thing you can do to a tree. It triggers weak regrowth, invites disease, and shortens the tree’s life by decades. If someone suggests topping your tree, that tells you everything you need to know about their training.

We perform crown thinning to improve light penetration and reduce wind load. Crown raising to clear structures, walkways, and sight lines. Deadwood removal to eliminate hazard branches before they fall. And structural pruning on young trees to prevent the co-dominant stems and included bark that cause failures 20 years down the road.

Winter is actually the best time to prune most species in our region. Trees are dormant, disease vectors like oak wilt and Dutch elm disease are inactive, and the bare canopy lets us see the full branch structure clearly. Bur oak, northern red oak, hackberry, linden, and most hardwoods all benefit from dormant-season pruning.

Pruning is one of those services where the difference between “someone who cuts trees” and a certified arborist becomes obvious. Proper pruning extends a tree’s life and improves its structure. Improper pruning — topping, lion’s tailing, over-thinning — causes damage that takes years to show and can never fully be undone. The cheapest bid on a pruning job is rarely the one that’s best for the tree.

Stump Grinding

The tree came down three years ago, but the stump is still there. You mow around it every week. You’ve tripped on it twice. The kids avoid that part of the yard. Every spring you think about getting it taken care of, and every fall it’s still sitting there. Meanwhile, the roots keep sending up suckers, and carpenter ants have started setting up camp.

When You Need This

  • Old stumps from previous removals — even if another company did the work
  • Stumps in the middle of your lawn, garden, or planned landscaping area
  • Root systems heaving sidewalks, driveways, or foundation walls
  • Stumps attracting insects, fungal growth, or sucker regrowth
  • Property cleanup before selling, building, or grading

What We Do

We grind stumps 6 to 8 inches below grade — deep enough to plant grass, lay sod, or build over. The grinding chips are used to backfill the hole, and we leave the area level and ready for whatever comes next. If you’d prefer clean fill instead of chip backfill, we can arrange that too.

For stumps near foundations, sidewalks, or utilities, we offer root grinding — following surface roots laterally to remove the sections causing damage without disturbing what’s around them. This requires equipment control and experience. A stump grinder in the wrong hands near a foundation wall or gas line is a liability, not a solution.

Stump-only jobs are always welcome. You don’t need a tree removal to get a stump ground — we show up for the stump and nothing else if that’s what you need. Every job gets the same attention, whether it’s one stump in a front yard or twenty in a development.

Some folks call around for stump grinding and never hear back. It’s a common frustration — smaller jobs tend to get ignored when a company is chasing bigger contracts. That’s a business model choice, not an inevitability. A company that prices fairly across the board — and actually returns every call — isn’t doing you a favor. They’re just running their business the way it should be run.

Shelter Belt Removals

That row of trees along the quarter section looked great when it was planted 40 years ago. Now the Siberian elms are half dead, the spruce are thinning from the bottom up, and the caragana has turned into an impenetrable wall of brush. The shelter belt did its job for decades — but now it’s creating more problems than it solves: blocked sight lines on the road, rodent habitat, and fire risk in dry summers.

When You Need This

  • Aging or dead shelter belts on farm and ranch land
  • County and township road clearing for visibility and safety compliance
  • New development sites where old windbreaks need to come out for construction
  • Government or DOT construction projects requiring full corridor clearing
  • Mixed-species rows (elm, spruce, caragana, ash) in any stage of decline

What We Do

Shelter belt removal is a start-to-finish operation. We bring the heavy equipment — feller bunchers to cut and stack, grapple trucks to sort and haul, and forestry mulchers to process brush and small-diameter wood on-site. For long rows with mixed species, we work systematically from one end to the other, keeping the site organized and the debris under control the entire time.

Siberian elm is brittle and unpredictable. Dead spruce snap under their own weight. Caragana is dense and thorny. Each species in a shelter belt presents its own challenge, and the crew needs to adjust technique constantly as they move through the row. This isn’t a one-size-fits-all operation.

When the trees are down, the site is clean. Stumps ground, brush chipped or mulched, logs hauled. The landowner or developer gets a cleared, usable corridor — not a debris field.

Shelter belt jobs are big, messy, and logistically demanding. They require real commercial equipment and a crew that knows how to run it efficiently. A company that specializes in tree work — not a landscaping outfit that occasionally takes on tree jobs — brings the right gear from day one. No scrambling to rent equipment, no learning on the job, no half-finished rows waiting for a return trip that may or may not happen.

Land & Lot Clearing

You’re looking at a piece of land and seeing what it could be — a new home, a commercial building, an expansion of something already in progress. But between you and that vision is an acre of overgrown trees, brush, and undergrowth that needs to be gone before the first survey stake goes in. You need it cleared right, cleared completely, and cleared on a timeline that doesn’t hold up the rest of the project.

When You Need This

  • Residential lot prep for new construction or subdivisions
  • Commercial and industrial site clearing
  • Expansion projects for existing properties — outbuildings, parking, drainage
  • Agricultural land reclamation from overgrown timber
  • Pre-grading clearing for road construction or utilities

What We Do

Forestry Mulching

You bought the acreage five years ago. The plan was to clean it up, maybe build on the back half, open up the views. But every summer the brush gets thicker, the volunteer trees get taller, and the property looks more like it’s reclaiming itself than the other way around. When You Need This

  • Overgrown acreage with dense brush, saplings, and small-diameter trees
  • Trail and path clearing through wooded property
  • Pasture reclamation where trees and brush have encroached
  • Fire mitigation around rural structures
  • Pre-development clearing where minimal ground disturbance is important

What We Do

A forestry mulcher is a single-pass machine that grinds standing trees, brush, and undergrowth into a layer of natural mulch right where it stands. No hauling, no burn piles, no exposed dirt. The mulch layer stays on the ground, preventing erosion and breaking down over time to improve the soil.

This method works best for trees up to about 8 inches in diameter and any amount of brush. For larger trees mixed in with the undergrowth, we fell them separately and process the rest with the mulcher. The result is a clean, walkable surface with intact topsoil underneath — ready for seeding, building, or just enjoying.

Compared to traditional clearing methods, forestry mulching causes dramatically less ground disturbance. No dozer tracks, no torn-up root balls, no piles of debris to deal with later. It’s one machine, one pass, and done.

Forestry mulching is specialized work that requires a specific machine and an operator who knows how to read terrain, avoid rocks and buried debris, and manage the mulch depth for the site conditions. It’s not a chainsaw job and it’s not a dozer job. Working with a company that owns the equipment and runs it regularly — rather than one that rents it for the occasional job — means fewer surprises, cleaner results, and a more accurate estimate upfront.
